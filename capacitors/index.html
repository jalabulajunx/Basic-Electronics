What are Capacitors?

Capacitors are components that store electrical energy, like tiny batteries! They can charge up and release their energy when needed. Think of them like water tanks that can fill up and empty out.

Understanding Capacitor Units

Farad (F)

The basic unit of capacitance. It's like measuring how much water a tank can hold!

💧

1 Farad = A really big capacitor (like a swimming pool!)

Microfarad (µF)

One millionth of a Farad. Much more common in everyday electronics!

🚰

1 µF = A small water bottle

💡

Common in LED lights and small toys

Nanofarad (nF)

One billionth of a Farad. Used in smaller circuits!

🥤

1 nF = A few drops of water

🎮

Found in game controllers and remote controls

Picofarad (pF)

One trillionth of a Farad. The smallest common unit!

💧

1 pF = A tiny drop of water

📱

Used in phones and computers

Charging and Discharging

Capacitors store energy by building up charge on their plates. The time it takes to charge depends on the capacitance value!

Series vs Parallel

In series: 1/Ctotal = 1/C1 + 1/C2

In parallel: Ctotal = C1 + C2

Fun Fact

Did you know? The first capacitors were called "Leyden jars" and were used to store static electricity!

How it Works:

  1. The choke (inductor) provides high voltage to start the tube and limits current during operation
  2. The starter contains a small gas discharge tube that helps initiate the main tube's glow
  3. The capacitor improves power factor by compensating for the inductive load
  4. When started, the gas inside the tube ionizes, creating a bright glow
